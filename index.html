About

I study how institutional frictions and investor behavior shape asset prices, with a focus on options markets. My job market paper shows that margin requirements and information asymmetry jointly generate an asymmetric price impact of demand in equity option markets — buy orders move implied volatility substantially more than equal-sized sell orders.

Job Market Paper

Job Market Paper
Asymmetric Demand-Based Option Pricing: Evidence from Margin Constraints and Information Asymmetry
with Sophie Xiaoyan Ni and Neil D. Pearson

Customer buy orders in equity option markets raise implied volatility significantly more than sell orders of equal size lower it. Using roughly 2.4 million stock-day observations (2015–2024), we estimate an asymmetric demand coefficient of 0.011 (Newey–West t = 16.16) that is pervasive across maturities and moneyness. We identify two complementary, jointly necessary channels. The margin-requirement channel concentrates informed order flow on the buy side: the asymmetry intensifies in funding-tight periods and for high-volatility stocks, and weakens for less capital-constrained professional and large-order traders. The information-asymmetry channel governs how aggressively market makers price that flow: the effect spikes to 0.197 (t = 3.70) on the earnings-announcement eve — about seventeen times the baseline — declines monotonically with analyst coverage, and vanishes for S&P 500 index options, where stock-specific private information does not exist.

Working Papers

Roses from the Stock Markets
Minor Revision · Pacific-Basin Finance Journal

Using Valentine’s Day as an instrument for sentiment in a setting where markets stay open, I document a robust Valentine effect in the US market (1927–2022): the three-day return around Valentine’s Day is 39 bps higher than around the 14th of other months and forecasts cumulative returns two to five months out. The effect appears across most European markets, is accompanied by post-holiday ETF outflows consistent with sentiment-driven overpricing, and is not explained by calendar, size, or announcement anomalies. A one-standard-deviation rise in the Twitter happiness index the day before Valentine’s Day maps to a 10 bps higher daily return over the surrounding window.

Predicting Bitcoin Returns with the GBTC Premium
Working Paper

I employ various statistical methods to examine whether the premium of the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC) over its net asset value predicts future Bitcoin returns, linking the premium to sentiment and arbitrage frictions in cryptocurrency markets.
